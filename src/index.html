Weight managment in college on KU campus

College is, for most people, a revolutionary experience in living away from home for the first time, and thus, your quality of life is completely dependent on yourself. When you're the only one you can rely on, you need to ensure that you do everything in your power to look and feel your best so that your college experience is one that you'll remember fondly.

Managing your weight or improving fitness/physical activity goes beyond the benefits to the health and longevity of your body, and include benefits such as better performance in class and homework. This website will give you a more comperehensive look as to what it means to be fit and how you can take small, yet effective steps to reaching your goals without spending all day at the gym.

We will start with a series of small things you can change about your dorm life and free time to improve fitness, then give you a list of resources and toold to take a more calculated and scientific approach to your weight, finally, we will teach you how to stay motivated to reach your goals in even the most dire of times. Along the way, we will explain all of the different benefits of these small changes you can make to your life that will develop into habits for success after you graduate.

First, what is a healthy body weight?

First of all, how are you even supposed to know if you're at a healthy and maintainable bodyweight or not? For the standard (non-athlete) person, the Body Mass Index (BMI) calculation is an effective tool for determining whether or not you are considered overweight. The BMI is simply your bodyeight in kilograms(kg) divided by your height in meters(m). To find out your BMI and whether or not it is healthy, see https://www.nhlbi.nih.gov/health/educational/lose_wt/BMI/bmicalc.htm (also available in our Sources and Additional Resource pages).

For athletes, the Fat-Free Mass Index (FFMI) may be a more appropriate tool for you to determine your current levels of muscularity compared to other men. The FFMI is similar to the BMI, but with the modification of using your Lean Body Mass as opposed to total body mass. Do note that the FFMI is not a perfect scale and may not reflect your muscularity accurately based on your body fat percentage (Loenneke et al.).

Neither of these calculations are required to begin working on your health and fitness, but they are a nice measure of progress and development as you grow into a full adult.

Next, understanding where the weight comes from...

To understand where bodyweight comes from, there's a few things you should know. The first principle is understanding the Law of Conservation of Energy, which states that all energy in a closed system is conserved. Well, your body IS a closed system in terms of energy, so any that you do not use, will be stored for later.\ Next is the Calorie, which is just a measurment of the energy you gain from eating food based on that food and the amount that you eat. Overeating or having a low level of activity will cause you to store excess calories (or energy) as body fat.

This means that you need to find a way to change your calorie balance to your benefit. So if you are looking to gain weight, you will need to eat more and lower your activity level, and if you want to lose it, you'll have to eat less and increase activity. If you need to calculate how many calories you should be eating a day (roughly), use this calculator https://www.calculator.net/calorie-calculator.html

Lastly, you need to understand that a calorie is a calorie, and that removing certain types of calories from your diet WILL NOT improve your weight unless you're lowering your overall caloric intake (Howell and Kones)!

Finally, some effotless tips on getting healthier and managing weight!

Tip #1: Always check the calorie count on your food and track what you eat in a day. It is much easier to reduce your caloric intake when you know how much you're eating

Tip #2: Get a full 8 hours of sleep. Sleep is an important part of not only your academic life, but also your health and wellness, so make sure you get more than 8 hours per night

Tip #3: We all know free time is hard to get in exercise, so walk or jog to class and back instead of taking the bus. a 180 pound person burns 100 calories just from walking a mile!

Tip #4: The University of Kansas offers free access to the David A. Ambler Student Recreation Fitness Center where you can work out on all sorts of qorkout equipment, run on the track, or play a game of basketball to ease stress and increase that activity level. So, go get those gains!
